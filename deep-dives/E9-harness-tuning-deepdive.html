0Why it matters

E1 gave this workbook its founding equation — Agent = Model + Harness (Böckeler) — and E3 showed what a harness is responsible for. Through late 2025 and 2026 the industry finished the thought: it named the practice, built a method for it, and produced the first hard evidence that the harness is not the junior partner in that equation. The name is harness engineering — attributed to Viv Trivedy of LangChain, and defined by HumanLayer as "the practice of leveraging configuration points to customize and improve your coding agent's output quality and reliability." The evidence is blunter than the definition: a May 2026 position paper measured that harness-induced variance can exceed model-induced variance by 7.8× on average — holding the model fixed and swapping only the scaffold moved SWE-bench-family scores by 9 to 48 points (arXiv:2605.23950). NVIDIA now runs a named "Harness Robustness" stage in post-training; LangChain took an open model to near-frontier eval scores at a tenth of the cost by touching only the harness; and Anthropic ships engineering playbooks for harness design. For our engagement this reframes the client's favourite question. "Which model should we buy?" is the wrong first question — the right one is which harness, configured how, and how will we measure our changes to it. This chapter gives that question a method: the tunable surface (§1), the evidence that tuning it moves outcomes (§2), the authoring discipline (§3), the eval-driven tuning loop (§4), and the co-evolution trap that makes harness choice a strategic decision rather than a convenience (§5).

1The tunable surface — anatomy of the thing you tune

You cannot tune what you cannot name. Two independent source-level analyses, published within weeks of each other, give the field its two reference architectures — and they disagree in an instructive way. The first (arXiv:2604.14228, "Dive into Claude Code," Apr 2026) dissects Claude Code and finds deliberate simplicity: "the core of the system is a simple while-loop that calls the model, runs tools, and repeats" — a single ReAct loop with a single state object, explicitly contrasted with graph-based orchestration, delegating to subagents by re-entering the same loop. The second (arXiv:2603.05344, "Building Effective AI Coding Agents for the Terminal," Mar 2026) describes OpenDev, an open-source terminal agent built as the opposite: a compound AI system — "not a single monolithic LLM, but a structured ensemble of agents and workflows," a four-level Session → Agent → Workflow → LLM hierarchy with five separately assignable model roles (execution, thinking, self-critique, vision, fallback). Both are non-peer-reviewed preprints, and OpenDev's description is its builder's own; but together they bound the design space our client will actually choose from: one loop, many levers versus many loops, many models.

Across both designs, the June 2026 survey that treats the harness as a first-class research object (arXiv:2606.20683) decomposes the execution harness into six runtime responsibilities — a useful checklist because every one of them is a place where configuration, not model choice, decides behaviour:

SURFACE 1–2

Observation & context

what the model sees

Tool outputs, file reads, error messages, and the assembled context window: system prompt, context files, skill metadata, tool descriptions. Everything M4 governs — progressive disclosure (I5), memory (I4) — is tuning surface here. This is where the LangChain "guidance placement" result of §4 lives.

SURFACE 3–4

Control & action

what the model may do

The loop itself: turn budgets, retry logic, loop caps, permission gates, hooks, subagent spawning; and the tool inventory — which tools exist, how they are described, what they return. HumanLayer's six levers (system prompts, tools/MCP, context, subagents, hooks, skills) are the practitioner cut of the same surface.

SURFACE 5–6

State & verification

what persists, what pushes back

Session state, checkpoints, progress files, memory writes; and the feedback machinery — tests, linters, typecheckers wired into the loop (E3/E4). Anthropic's long-running-agent harnesses put their most load-bearing rules here (the feature-list protection rule I2 verified; the initializer/coding-agent split of its Nov 2025 playbook).

A practitioner vocabulary worth borrowing sits one level up. Alejandro Piad Morffis ("The Anatomy of AI Coding Agents," Apr 2026 — an informed practitioner synthesis with no benchmarks, from a University of Havana professor who builds his own OpenCode tooling) separates Mode ("the persona the AI adopts… it determines what the agent can do, not just how it thinks" — enforced permissions, not tone), Skill (knowledge "applied implicitly when necessary"), Command (user-invoked workflow: "do this, in this order, using these tools") and Subagent (ephemeral delegation that "returns a summarized result so the main context stays clean"). The explicit-versus-implicit axis — modes and commands are invoked, skills self-activate — is a sharper framing than most tool docs offer, and it maps almost one-to-one onto Claude Code's primitives. His context-budget heuristic — target 40–60% saturation rather than riding to the ~85% compaction threshold — is opinion, but it is the same direction E2's context-rot evidence points.

2Same model, different harness — the evidence

The claim that the harness dominates would be vendor marketing if only harness vendors made it. What changed in 2026 is that it acquired an evidence base with independent entries. The strongest is the position paper "Stop Comparing LLM Agents Without Disclosing the Harness" (arXiv:2605.23950, May 2026). Its authors ran a controlled factorial experiment — three models (GPT-5.4, Kimi K2.6, GLM-5.1) crossed with three harnesses on a SWE-bench Verified subset — and found harness-induced variance exceeding model-induced variance by 7.80× on average. Their collected observational numbers are just as stark: holding Claude Opus 4.5 fixed, SEAL versus Claude Code differ by 9.5 points on SWE-bench Pro; on the HAL leaderboard's SWE-bench Verified Mini, the same Claude Sonnet 4.5 scores 68% under SWE-Agent and 34% under HAL's generalist scaffold — a 34-point swing from scaffolding alone, with a ~48-point swing for o4-mini. Their conclusion is the sentence we should hand the client's evaluation committee verbatim: "Until harness specifications are disclosed, leaderboard comparisons for long-horizon agents should be treated as incomplete and potentially misleading."

The model-builder side confirms it from within. NVIDIA's Nemotron 3 Ultra technical report (arXiv:2606.15007) shows the same model spanning 65–70.4% on SWE-bench Verified depending on which of five harnesses runs it, and its official reproducibility recipe pins not just the model but the harness and sampling parameters — OpenHands for SWE-bench, Terminus-2 for Terminal-Bench, temperature, top-p and turn limits all named — because without them the number is not reproducible. Terminal-Bench itself (a Stanford × Laude collaboration) makes the separation mechanical: agents register as adapters via its Harbor framework, and the CLI takes --agent and --model as independent flags. And Artificial Analysis, benchmarking Nemotron's launch, went a step further — it customized the Terminus-2 harness so that "every run states the turn budget up front… and warns the model as it approaches the limit," on the reasoning that "silently truncating an agent at N turns penalizes models that didn't know the limit was coming." A third-party benchmarker editing the harness to remove harness-induced bias is the practice of §4 arriving in the measurement industry (disclosed caveat: the analysis was NVIDIA-partnered for pre-release access).

The Terminal-Bench story is two stories — keep them apart

Practitioner posts blur two separate results into one meme. Result one (HumanLayer, Mar 2026): Claude Opus 4.6 placed "#33 in Claude Code… #5 in a different harness that wasn't seen during post-training (+/- about 4 positions)" on Terminal-Bench 2.0 — an Anthropic model, cited to argue post-training overfitting (§5). Result two (Viv Trivedy, LangChain, Feb 2026): "we… iteratively improve[d] deepagents-cli 13.7 points from 52.8 to 66.5 on Terminal Bench 2.0. We only tweaked the harness and kept the model fixed, gpt-5.2-codex" — the result Osmani retells as "Top 30 to Top 5." Different models, different teams, same moral. Both are self-reported and point-in-time — the live leaderboard has long since moved on — so cite them as illustrations of harness sensitivity, never as current rankings.

The same discipline applies at the small end of the surface. The ETH Zurich context-file evaluation this workbook leans on in I5 (arXiv:2602.11988) is a harness-tuning result in miniature: adding LLM-generated context files — a one-file harness change — reduced resolution rates (−0.5% SWE-bench Lite, −2% on the authors' CTXBENCH) while raising cost by +20%/+23%, and developer-written files bought only +2.4%, short of significance. Tuning the surface can move outcomes in both directions; that is precisely why §4's loop measures before it keeps. The evidence table, tiered per our house rules:

3The discipline — the Ratchet and the six levers

If §2 establishes that the surface matters, the discipline question is how to work it without thrashing. The founding move is attributed to Mitchell Hashimoto (mitchellh.com, Feb 2026): "anytime you find an agent makes a mistake, you take the time to engineer a solution such that the agent never makes that mistake again." Osmani names the resulting dynamic the Ratchet and gives it teeth as a provenance rule: "Every line in a good AGENTS.md should be traceable back to a specific thing that went wrong." That rule is quietly radical, and it converges with what this workbook found independently: I5's gardening budget said every always-loaded line must be load-bearing; the Ratchet says every line must also have a failure as its birth certificate. A CLAUDE.md line you cannot trace to an incident is a candidate for deletion — and a ratcheted rule that matters should graduate from advisory prose to a deterministic check, which is exactly this repo's own CLAUDE.md-versus-validate.py split (E5's advisory/deterministic distinction, applied to the harness itself).

Two more design laws recur across the practitioner sources, both context-economic in origin. First, "success is silent, failures are verbose" — hooks and verification should say nothing when things pass and speak in detail when they fail. HumanLayer's war story makes it concrete: wiring the full test suite to run after every change flooded the context with 4,000 lines of passing output, and the agent lost the task and hallucinated. The verification machinery E4 builds must be context-efficient or it becomes the failure mode it guards against. Second, subagents as a context firewall — delegate discrete work (locate a definition, trace a flow, run a research errand) so intermediate noise never enters the parent thread, returning condensed answers with filepath:line citations. HumanLayer pairs this with a deliberately contrarian stance: persona-based subagent splits ("frontend engineer," "backend engineer") are ineffective theatre — the value of a subagent is context isolation, not roleplay. That is one vendor's doctrine, not settled fact, but it is a useful challenge to cargo-cult multi-agent setups — and note it targets persona splits, not role separation in the planner/generator/evaluator sense, which Anthropic's own harness work supports (§4).

On attribution, one correction to the sources themselves: HumanLayer credits "context engineering" to its cofounder Dex Horthy. The broader record says the term was popularized by Andrej Karpathy's June 2025 formulation ("the delicate art and science of filling the context window with just the right information for the next step"), with Horthy's 12-factor-agents an influential early articulation. Harness engineering sits inside context engineering, which subsumes prompt engineering — E2's hierarchy, now with named layers.

4Eval-driven tuning — the method

The genuinely new material of 2026 is not any single lever but a method for pulling them: treat the harness as the system under test, and let a task-level eval suite — not vibes — decide what changes survive. LangChain's Nemotron playbook (Jul 2026) is the most explicit published loop, run against its 127-test Deep Agents suite (five categories: files, tools, retrieval, conversation, summarization):

STEP 1–2

Evaluate, then read the traces

diagnosis before prescription

Run the suite; then read the failing trajectories and name the recurring failure pattern. The unit of diagnosis is the behaviour class ("stops reading files at the pagination boundary"), not the failing test.

STEP 3–4

One change, up the cost ladder

cheap screen, expensive confirm

Make one targeted change and re-run — first on a small cheap eval, promoting to the full expensive suite only when the win survives. The ladder mirrors this repo's own spot-verify pattern: screen with a fast check, confirm with a thorough one.

STEP 5

Keep only what repeats — and nothing regresses

the ratchet, quantified

A change is kept when the improvement repeats across trials and no other category regresses. Keep behaviour-class fixes; reject "benchmark tricks" that exploit specific test phrasing — the difference between tuning and overfitting.

Two of the playbook's specific findings deserve to travel. Guidance placement beats guidance content: a "keep reading" instruction did nothing in a tool's description, and started working the moment it moved into the tool's return value — the point in the trajectory where the model was actually failing. Broad system-prompt rewrites, by contrast, "wash out"; short targeted blocks aimed at an observed failure work. That is E2's attention economics operationalized. And the diagnostic boundary: after tuning, four of five categories improved but conversation stayed flat — which the authors read correctly as a model limitation, not a harness one. A category that will not move under harness changes tells you to stop tuning and change (or wait for) the model. That single heuristic — flat under tuning = model problem; moving = configuration problem — is the cleanest available answer to "should we keep engineering or switch vendors?"

The headline result, tiered honestly: harness tuning took Nemotron 3 Ultra from ~0.80 to ~0.84 typical (best 0.86, near Opus 4.8's 0.87) at "roughly 10x cheaper per run… about $4.48 against $43.48 on the full suite." The companion NVIDIA post supplies the one ablated data point in the whole story: a single middleware (a pagination notice appended to read_file output) fixed all three failing file tests and moved the suite from 94 to 96/127. All of it is vendor-run on the vendor's own eval — no arXiv paper, no external replication — which is why §2's independent anchors matter. But the method is corroborated beyond LangChain: Braintrust markets "eval-driven development" as its core practice ("evaluations serve as the working specification"), and Anthropic's harness-design post for long-running app development (Mar 2026) reports the same loop from the vendor side — a GAN-inspired generator/evaluator split (because models grade their own work too generously — E4's evaluator ≠ generator, again), with iteration driving cost per run from ~6 hours/$200 to ~3.8 hours/$125. Eval-driven harness tuning is a practice with multiple independent practitioners, even if every published number is still a vendor claim.

5Co-evolution — when the model learns its harness

The deepest 2026 development is that the coupling now runs both ways: harnesses are tuned to models, and models are post-trained inside harnesses. The Nemotron 3 Ultra report makes it explicit and named: "Harness Robustness is a key part of the post-training stage… we ensure that the model is trained under at least two of the following harnesses: Stirrup, OpenHands, OpenCode, Terminus, Droid, Custom Internal Harnesses," with RLVR data built from "a diverse collection of harness implementations and interaction formats… reducing overfitting to any particular harness design." Read that from the other side: overfitting to a training harness is a real enough failure mode that a frontier lab budgets a named training stage against it. It is the most parsimonious explanation for HumanLayer's Opus-4.6 observation in §2 — a model can be strongest inside the harness it grew up in — and Osmani generalizes it into a co-evolution thesis: better models do not shrink the harness, they shift which scaffolding matters; "anxiety-mitigation" components fall away while higher-level orchestration grows.

Three practical consequences for our engagement. First, benchmark hygiene: a published agentic score is a property of a (model, harness, parameters) triple — when the client's evaluation matrix quotes SWE-bench numbers, require the harness column (§2's position paper gives the citation). Second, vendor strategy (T6): if models perform best in their native harnesses and harnesses encode your accumulated Ratchet rules, then switching costs live in the harness layer, not the model API. The emerging "Harness-as-a-Service" pattern Osmani describes — agent SDKs (Claude Agent SDK, Codex SDK, OpenAI Agents SDK) that ship the loop, tool-calling, state and hooks as a configurable runtime — is the buy-side answer: you configure a maintained loop instead of rebuilding one, and your harness engineering concentrates in the portable layers (context files, evals, hooks, skills). Third, the pruning principle survives: E3 argued a good harness shrinks as models improve; co-evolution refines that — it shrinks in different places than it grows, and only an eval suite tells you which is which. All tool-specific facts in this section are point-in-time (mid-2026) and re-verify before client use.

What we do not claim

Two refuted claims circulate with this material; neither belongs in client decks. LangChain/NVIDIA "harness profiles" are not "first of their kind" — per-model harness adaptation predates the branding. And the viral "only 1.6% of Claude Code's codebase is AI decision logic" figure traces to an unnamed "community analysis" cited secondhand in arXiv:2604.14228 — no primary source, do not cite. The 7.80× variance figure, meanwhile, is one factorial experiment in a preprint: strong, plausibly the most important number in this chapter, and still awaiting peer review and replication. Tier accordingly.

6Week one at the client

Harness engineering is unusually easy to start because the first artifacts are small. Day one — inventory the surface. Which harness(es) are actually in use (IDE, CLI, CI); which of §1's six responsibilities each one exposes as configuration; who owns each lever today (nobody, usually — that is finding one, and T5's internal-product argument). Day two — seed the eval. Collect 10–20 real failed or painful agent tasks from the team's history — not synthetic puzzles — and turn them into a runnable suite with pass criteria. This is the client's Deep-Agents-suite-in-miniature, and it doubles as the pilot's measurement instrument (E7/T2: the agent-level eval complements DORA's team-level metrics; neither substitutes for the other). Day three onward — run the loop. One change at a time up the cost ladder; every kept change logged with the failure that motivated it (the Ratchet, providing I3-style provenance for the harness itself); every incident that recurs twice becomes a candidate for promotion from CLAUDE.md prose to a deterministic hook (E5). Week's end — the boundary check: anything flat after honest tuning goes on the "model limitation" list that feeds the vendor conversation, with §2's disclosure rule attached to any benchmark the vendor quotes back.

8Applying it — decision guide

Three sentences to keep

An agentic benchmark score is a property of model plus harness — demand the harness column before believing any number, because measured harness-induced variance can exceed model-induced variance several-fold. Tune the harness the way you refactor code: one change at a time, against an eval suite built from your own failures, keeping only wins that repeat — and read a category that stays flat as a model limit, not a configuration one. Give every always-loaded rule a failure as its birth certificate, and promote the rules that matter from prose to deterministic checks.
