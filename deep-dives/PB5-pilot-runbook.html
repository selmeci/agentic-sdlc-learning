0Why this runbook exists

PB5 closes the journey: PB1 measured the ground, PB2 built the net, PB3 built the cage, PB4 ran the contract once — PB5 decides, with numbers, whether the whole construction earns wider scope. It is B6's F2 phase run as an experiment: DORA supplies the metrics and E7's METR-style method supplies the honesty — a frozen baseline, an as-random-as-possible comparison, success criteria written down before the first pilot task — and B6's F2→F3 gate with its back-triggers and STOP criteria supplies the decision. The exit is deliberately concrete: a signed GO / HOLD / STOP decision against pre-registered criteria, in a report the client can hand to their board.

Like PB3 and PB4, this runbook has exactly one entry posture: a recorded PB4 GO — the handoff-delivery report proving the discipline ran end-to-end once. Two earlier artifacts are inherited by reference: PB1's frozen delivery baseline (assessment report §3 — the pilot's "before" snapshot, to which this runbook finally adds the token column PB1 left open) and the running harness itself — PB2's net and PB3's cage stay on throughout, because the pilot measures the system, not the model: an agent with the discipline switched off is a different system, and its numbers transfer to nothing. No PB4 GO → the loop has not run even once; there is nothing to scale — go back to PB4 Handoff.

The failure PB5 prevents is the best-measured one in this workbook: believing feel. The METR RCT (16 experienced developers, 246 real tasks on their own mature repos, AI use randomized per task, arXiv:2507.09089) measured developers 19% slower with early-2025 AI while they believed they were ~20% faster — having predicted +24% going in. The wider business statistics say the same thing at organisation scale, once read correctly: MIT NANDA's much-misquoted report actually says "95% of organizations are getting zero measurable return" on enterprise GenAI (July 2025, not peer-reviewed; 300+ initiative reviews, 52 interviews, 153 surveys — the circulating "95% of pilots fail" is a misquote), and KPMG's 2026 stall diagnosis is blunter: enterprise AI does not stall because pilots fail — it stalls because the pilot moved fast "precisely because governance was minimal, data was curated, scope was controlled." PB1–PB4 exist so that this pilot is not that pilot: the governance, the data discipline and the scope were installed first, which is exactly what makes its result transferable.

The control arm is now the scarce resource

The field's flagship study broke on this in 2026, and said so: METR's follow-up (57 developers, 800+ tasks, late-2025 tools) found the original cohort still −18% (CI −38%…+9%) but new developers only −4% (CI −15%…+9%) — and METR itself rates the result "very weak evidence", because 30–50% of developers withheld tasks they did not want to do without AI, and the experiment is being redesigned (metr.org, 2026-02-24; E7 §4 tells the full story). The lesson lands directly on this runbook: by mid-2026, developers' unwillingness to work without AI is the biggest design risk in any pilot. Protect the control arm or lose the experiment — assignment is never self-selected, defections are logged, and a contaminated comparison is reported as contaminated, not spun.

What this is not

Not a surveillance program: every delivery metric here is system/team-level, never a per-developer scorecard — the moment DORA keys become individual targets, Goodhart's law destroys the signal (E7). Not a dashboard purchase: all of it is computable from git history, CI logs and the incident system the client already has; telemetry products are optional and their vendor numbers are marketing until reproduced locally. And not a proof machine: with dozens of tasks per arm — a realistic client sample — the honest output is often direction, not percentages, and this runbook says so out loud rather than quoting false precision.

The theory this runbook distils: E7 for the DORA process (the four keys plus rework rate, computed from data that already exists), the METR method (randomize what you can, measure objectively, pre-register success, state caveats) and the anti-pattern catalogue; B6 for the F2→F3 gate, the back-triggers and the STOP criteria — and the standing flag that F0–F3 and its thresholds are this workbook's Report 4 synthesis, not an industry standard. PB5 is also the seed of the T2 deliverable (measurement & pilot design), the trajectory topic this material converges with.

1How to run it

Run PB5 with one person plus the pilot team, the requirement owner keeping PB4's loop alive, and the client sponsor — who signs the pre-registration and receives the report. Scope: the F2 write scope PB3 granted (the heatmap-selected modules), one pilot window. Duration is a floor, not a target: 8–12 weeks per window and dozens of comparable tasks per arm (E7's heuristic); below that floor, claim direction only. Steps are written as ČO (the action), PREČO (the theory link) and DONE (the exit criterion).

PB3's probe rule has a measurement twin here: a metric pipeline that has not run once on real data is not installed. Phase A therefore ends with a dry run — every metric computed once on a week of existing history before the window you will bet on. What cannot be probed — whether the client will accept a STOP recommendation — is exactly the judgment this runbook exists to put in front of them, in writing.

In the workbook version of this runbook, progress is saved locally (same store as topic progress). This standalone copy does not persist checkbox state.

2Phase A — Pre-registration: freeze the bar before the race

Phase A is where the pilot is won or lost, and no pilot task runs until it is done. Everything in it exists to make the end-of-window conversation short: the baseline is frozen, the comparison is designed, the success bar and the kill criteria are signed — so the numbers, when they arrive, land on a decision that was already agreed.

Re-run PB1's §3 queries over the agreed before-window and freeze both the numbers and the queries — commit them together, because identical queries before and after are what make a delta mean anything (E7 §2). Then answer this topic's open question in writing: for each of the five keys (deploy frequency, lead time, change-fail rate, recovery time, rework rate) the client cannot compute from day 0, record the named proxy that replaces it — release tags standing in for deploy logs, hotfix-PR counts standing in for an absent incident system — and use the same proxy on both windows. A baseline captured at assess time and never re-pulled drifts: PB1 froze the snapshot, this step proves it still computes.

DONE = baseline table + the exact queries committed; every key either computed or replaced by a named proxy used identically on both windows.

Extend the baseline with the token column: measured token/usage spend per merged PR and per completed task, next to PB1's review-hours, CI-minutes and seat-licence columns (template below). The unit economics to watch is cost-to-merge, not token price: a July 2026 practitioner benchmark that held the merge gate constant measured $5.56–$69.97 per merged feature across models and found the two "point in opposite directions" — cheap models burn the difference in the review gate. And keep the column in its place: DX's Core 4 guidance (June 2026) files AI adoption and token spend under diagnostic telemetry, not outcomes — the cost column exists so ROI is reported next to the DORA deltas, never instead of them. Pricing churns monthly (Copilot moved to usage-based billing on 2026-06-01) — every price is point-in-time.

DONE = cost baseline computed on the before-window; the per-PR/per-task attribution rule written; token metering confirmed working for the pilot window.

Split comparable work into an agent arm and a control arm, as random as the work allows: per-task randomization like METR's if the stream permits, two comparable task streams or teams if not (E7 §3). Fix the unit of analysis — a team or module, never individuals — and put the contamination rules in writing: control tasks genuinely run without agents, assignment is never self-selected, defections are logged rather than punished. Include skeptics and late adopters in the agent arm — volunteer-only cohorts flatter the tool. The §0 callout is the reason this step is no longer optional politeness: METR's own follow-up collapsed on self-selection, with 30–50% of developers withholding tasks they did not want to do without AI. The closer to random, the more the result means; and how far from random you landed is itself a line in the report, not a secret.

DONE = assignment rule + contamination rules committed; both arms' task pools listed; the contamination log exists (and is empty).

Write the one-page pre-registration (template below): primary outcomes with thresholds or directions, the minimum sample, the guardrail bounds, and the back-trigger each breach turns — signed by the sponsor and module owner before the first pilot task, together with the sealed answers to the survey's first question (predicted speedup). Pre-registration is what makes the result unarguable afterwards (E7 §3); agreeing back-triggers now is what makes demotion automatic later — a number acting, not a meeting being won (B6 §5, borrowing the Digital Apprentice's asymmetry: promotion needs evidence plus a recorded human authorization, demotion happens without waiting for one — arXiv:2606.04321, a design proposal, flagged as such). The guardrail set follows the 2026 evidence to where the strain lands first: an enterprise "2× mandate" study (802 developers, 196,212 PRs, arXiv:2607.01904) watched per-capita throughput reach 2.09× the pre-mandate baseline while per-reviewer load roughly doubled and automated review overtook human review — merge and revert rates held, and the queue quietly became the constraint. Close the phase with the dry run: compute every metric once on a week of existing history, so the pipeline is proven before the window that counts.

DONE = pre-registration committed and signed; predicted-speedup answers sealed; dry run produced every metric once without hand-editing.

3Phase B — Run the measured window

Phase B is deliberately the quiet phase: the pilot team works, the telemetry accumulates, and the only weekly ceremony is fifteen minutes of guardrail reading. The discipline is in what does not happen — no mid-window autonomy promotions, no peeking at the primary outcomes to re-argue the bar, no switching the harness off to go faster.

Pilot tasks flow through PB4's contract loop at PB3's granted autonomy — and stay there: no promotions mid-window, because changing the treatment mid-experiment forfeits the comparison. Keep batches small: DORA 2024's mechanism for teams that kept stability while adopting AI is adherence to "small batch sizes and robust testing", and its data says batch size tends to increase when AI is used — resist exactly that drift. The restraint has a practitioner name: Swarmia's five-levels analysis puts the honest industry ceiling between levels 2 and 3, "and the reason is usually not the tool, but how purposefully they're using it" — the pilot exists to earn the next level with evidence, not to demo it early (E6).

DONE = pilot underway; REQ ids on every pilot PR (PB4's gate keeps proving it); zero autonomy changes logged mid-window.

Fifteen minutes weekly over the guardrail row (template below): rework direction, change-fail, review latency, PR size, queue depth, no-review-merge share, token spend. A fired trigger turns its agreed dial the same week — shrink batch, hold scope, fewer agents, module back to F1 — and the log records either the dial or the written reason it did not turn. The primary outcomes are not read mid-window; that is the pre-registration discipline holding. Why weekly, and why these: the failure shape arrives in the review queue first and fast — Faros' 2026 telemetry (vendor, ~22,000 developers, flagged as such) has median review time up 441.5%, PR size up 51.3% and 31.3% more PRs merging without any review; the independent 2×-mandate study confirms the mechanism at 802 developers with reviewer load doubling while merge and revert rates stayed flat — the strain hides in the queue, not in the defect count, until review is skipped (E7 AP3). Weekly is the cadence at which a dial still beats a crisis.

DONE = weekly log committed every week; every fired trigger followed by its dial or a written reason — none silently ignored.

During the window, do the two things 2026's field data says move adoption: make the champion's wins visible to peers — Microsoft's instrumented study of its own CLI-agent rollout (tens of thousands of engineers, arXiv:2607.01418) found first use spreading primarily through social networks and recommends exactly that lever — and run structured enablement rather than licence distribution: DX's Q4 2025 report (135,000 developers, 425 organizations) is blunt that "simply handing out licenses is not enough." Keep the two headline PR numbers separate when the client quotes them back: Microsoft's ~24% sustained merge lift and Faros' figures come from different methods and populations — never merge them into one number (B6 §3). At window end, collect the survey's second half (perceived speedup) before anyone sees the measured results — the gap between the two is a deliverable, evidence about bias that pre-empts "but it feels faster" in the gate meeting.

DONE = enablement actions logged; both survey halves collected, the second before results were shown; friction notes filed.

4Phase C — Read, gate, decide

Phase C is one honest computation and one signed decision. Everything hard was done in Phase A; what remains is refusing to soften it now that the numbers are real.

Recompute every pre-registered metric with the frozen queries; report per-metric deltas, stability always beside throughput, never a composite score (E7 §2). If the sample came in under the pre-registered minimum, the claims are direction-only — write "throughput moved up, stability held", not a percentage. Then compute the perception gap: predicted vs perceived vs measured. The reading rule comes from DORA's own arc: 2024 measured AI adoption costing stability (−7.2%) while 2025's report found throughput turned positive with instability still elevated — faster, but not safer, the amplifier thesis. A throughput gain with falling stability is not a partial win to round up; it is the signature of a weak surrounding system, and surfacing it is what separates this pilot from the vendor decks the client has already seen.

DONE = results table committed: every pre-registered metric, its delta (or direction), and the perception gap — same queries as the baseline, verified by diff.

Walk the gate checklist (template in §5) with named signatories. GO = pre-registered success met and guardrails within bounds → F3 expansion opens. HOLD = mixed or underpowered → extend the window or shrink scope and re-run the gate on a named date; HOLD is a normal exit, not a failure — the METR follow-up teaches that even the field's best measurement sometimes says "we cannot tell yet", and saying it is the credible move. STOP = a B6 STOP condition surfaced (no verification substrate, safety-critical scope without provenance, no audit trail) or a guardrail breached persistently with its dials exhausted → the recommendation is written down, because a roadmap that cannot say no is a sales document (B6 §5). Gates are decided by checkable facts, not the quarter ending: every row of the checklist is a number against a bound that was agreed in Phase A — the only judgment left is which of the three words to sign.

DONE = decision recorded with date, criteria and signatories; HOLD carries its re-run date; STOP names its condition.

Fill the report (template in §5): baseline-vs-pilot table, the perception gap, the guardrail log, cost per merged PR including tokens, the signed decision, and the caveats stated plainly — sample size, brownfield messiness, task heterogeneity; naming the limits is what makes the findings that survive them persuasive (E7 §3). On GO, the report carries the expansion plan: F3 proceeds module by module in heatmap order, and every new module gets the F1 treatment first — PB2's net and PB3's cage are recycled per module, promotions stay dual-key, demotion thresholds carry over (B6). The report has one more customer: this methodology itself. Its corpus is 100% externally cited — a finished pilot is the first first-party evidence, and the anonymized results entry feeding back into these deep dives is the field-report loop the roadmap reserves for exactly this artifact.

DONE = report delivered to the sponsor; on GO, the first expansion module named with its F1 checklist opened; the anonymized field-report entry drafted.

Exit routing

PB5 is the journey's last runbook — deliberately not the engagement's end. GO does not graduate the client out of the runbooks; it recycles them: every F3 module enters through PB2's net and PB3's cage again, in heatmap order, with the pilot's thresholds carried over — expansion that skips the per-module F1 treatment is the unverified-write failure B1 catalogued, at scale. HOLD re-runs this runbook's Phase C on a named date with a longer window or tighter scope. STOP is the recommendation you must be able to make — it is also the rarest, because PB1's F0 gate should have caught never-start conditions before a pilot was ever proposed; a STOP here usually means something changed mid-engagement, and the report should say what. Whichever way it goes: the anonymized results feed the field-report loop — the first first-party evidence in a corpus that has run on external citations until now.

Three sentences to remember PB5 by

The pilot is B6's F2 phase run as an experiment: a frozen baseline, an as-random-as-possible split with a protected control arm, and success criteria signed before the first task — because the best RCT in the field measured developers 19% slower while they believed they were 20% faster, and feel is not evidence. Guardrails are dials, not debates: review latency, rework and the no-review-merge share carry agreed back-trigger thresholds, and demotion happens because a number moved, never because a meeting was won. The gate has three honest exits — GO recycles PB2/PB3 per module into F3, HOLD extends the measurement, STOP is the recommendation a roadmap must be able to make — and whichever fires, the report is the methodology's first first-party evidence.
