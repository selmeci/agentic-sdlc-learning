0Why it matters

B1 set the safe ordering, B2 built the characterization net, B3 graded it, and B4 mapped the estate. Sooner or later the engagement has to answer the question every client actually asks: where do we start changing things? Pick wrong and the program dies in its first quarter — a failed pilot on a critical module poisons agent adoption for years; pick right and each strangled slice funds the next. The pattern language for doing this deliberately is old and battle-tested: the strangler fig (Fowler, 2004), seams (Feathers, 2004), the anti-corruption layer (Evans, 2003) and a hotspot heatmap mined from version-control history (Tornhill, 2015). What is new in 2026 is the economics around them: agents make the map cheap, make drive-by fixes seductive, and make the discipline of picking the battlefield more load-bearing, not less. This chapter holds one line: you do not modernize a legacy estate, you strangle it slice by slice — and the heatmap, not enthusiasm, decides which slice is first.

1The rewrite question, honestly framed

Every brownfield engagement starts with someone proposing the big-bang rewrite, so the case against it should be stated precisely. The canonical text is Joel Spolsky's Things You Should Never Do, Part I (6 April 2000), written the week Netscape 6 shipped its first beta: "They did it by making the single worst strategic mistake that any software company can make: They decided to rewrite the code from scratch." His numbers are timeline facts, not estimates — version 4.0 shipped in mid-1997, the rewrite delivered three years later, and "during this time, Netscape sat by, helplessly, as their market share plummeted… You are giving a gift of two or three years to your competitors." Brooks named the psychological mechanism a generation earlier (The Mythical Man-Month, 1975, ch. 5): the second system is "the most dangerous system a man ever designs" — the rewrite becomes the dumping ground for every deferred wish, and scope metastasizes exactly when the team has stopped shipping.

Honesty then demands three corrections to the folklore. First, there is no credible failure-rate number. The "X% of rewrites fail" figures circulating in vendor decks trace back to the Standish CHAOS reports, whose figures were dismantled by Eveleens & Verhoef (The Rise and Fall of the Chaos Report Figures, IEEE Software 27(1), 2010: small, self-selected samples, unclear definitions, irreproducible results) and by Jørgensen & Moløkken-Østvold (IST 48(4), 2006). The evidence base for "never do a big-bang rewrite" is case studies and expert judgment — strong, but print it as such and refuse to quote a percentage. Second, the famous counterexample is not actually a counterexample. DHH's REWRITE! talk (Business of Software, 2015) describes Basecamp 2 and 3 as from-scratch rewrites — but the old versions were never sunset ("keep using them until the end of the internet!"), which makes each rewrite a parallel new product coexisting with the legacy one: strangler economics wearing a rewrite's clothes. The sharpest version of the argument is his: the true worst strategic mistake is forced migration, because it makes every existing user re-evaluate you. FreshBooks' undercover "BillSpring" rewrite (documented in Herb Caudill's six-case survey Rewrite, refactor, or reinvent?, 2019) shows a deliberate rewrite can work — as a new product with an MVP deadline, not as in-place replacement. Third, "never rewrite" has its own failure mode. The same survey covers Spolsky's own FogBugz: two decades of never rewriting produced Wasabi, a proprietary in-house language, and a technical-debt bill that came due around 2010. The axis that actually predicts disaster is not rewrite-vs-refactor; it is risk concentration — one irreversible cutover versus many reversible slices. The strangler fig is the pattern that keeps every step reversible.

2The strangler fig pattern

The pattern's origin is more specific than the folklore. In June 2004 Martin Fowler published a short bliki post, originally titled StranglerApplication, after a trip with his wife Cindy to the Queensland rain forests, whose strangler vines "seed in the upper branches of a fig tree and gradually work their way down the tree until they root in the soil." The software proposal: "gradually create a new system around the edges of the old, letting it grow slowly over several years until the old system is strangled." Two attribution details keep the story honest: Fowler explicitly credits his colleague Chris Stevenson, whose team had just published the approach at XP 2004 (Stevenson & Pols, An Agile Approach to a Legacy System) — so the pattern was named from a working case study, not invented at a whiteboard; and the current page is a later, expanded rewrite — retitled Strangler Fig over, in Fowler's footnote, worries about "connotations of violence", with the deadpan punchline that "the new title strangled the old one." The expanded version folds the pattern into a four-activity modernization framework (understand the outcomes, break the problem up, deliver the parts, change the organization — credited to Ian Cartwright, Rob Horn and James Lewis) and a full Patterns of Legacy Displacement article series. Fowler's own summary of why the pattern matters is the sentence to quote in steering committees: the most important reason to consider a strangler is reduced risk — each increment ships, is verified, and can be abandoned without having bet the estate.

The mechanics are simple to state and easy to get wrong. An interception layer — an HTTP reverse proxy, a message router, sometimes just a facade class — sits in front of the legacy system. New and migrated functionality is built in the new system; the facade routes each call to whichever side owns it. Slice by slice the new system grows around the old, until the monolith has no traffic left and can be decommissioned — and then the facade itself, Microsoft's architecture guidance pointedly warns, must not become "a single point of failure or a performance bottleneck", nor a permanent fixture unless you deliberately keep it as an adapter for legacy clients. Fowler's 2004 post closes with the sentence every modernization program should pin above its desk: "all we are doing is writing tomorrow's legacy software today" — the strangler is not a way to reach a final architecture, it is a way to keep migrating continuously.

Why this pattern fits agents specifically

The strangler's unit of work — one slice, routed, verified against pinned behaviour, reversible — is exactly the unit of work an agent can be trusted with under B2's net and B3's gate. Big-bang rewrites fail with humans partly because verification is deferred to the end; an agent-accelerated big-bang defers the same verification at ten times the code-production speed. The pattern is not made obsolete by agents; it is the harness that makes their speed usable on an estate that cannot be allowed to break.

3Seams and the plumbing toolbox

The strangler fig says where the new system grows; Michael Feathers supplies the vocabulary for where you can cut. A seam, in Working Effectively with Legacy Code (2004), is "a place where you can alter behavior in your program without editing in that place" — with the companion notion of an enabling point, the spot where the choice between behaviours is made. Feathers catalogs preprocessing, link and object seams; Fowler's Legacy Seam bliki (January 2024) explicitly extends the idea from its testing origin to legacy displacement — the same cut that lets you pin behaviour in a test harness (B2) lets you route it to a new implementation (B5). The operational catch, named in our topic notes as "seams are unicorns", is real: many modules have no natural seam, and then the first move is not extraction but seam manufacture — small, net-protected refactorings that create an enabling point where none existed.

Four named patterns do the plumbing. Anti-corruption layer (Evans, Domain-Driven Design, 2003, ch. 14): "Create an isolating layer to provide clients with functionality in terms of their own domain model. The layer talks to the other system through its existing interface, requiring little or no modification to the other system." During coexistence the new system will have to call unmigrated legacy behaviour; the ACL is what stops the legacy model's vocabulary and invariants from leaking into — and eventually dictating — the new design. Branch by abstraction: when the thing being replaced is deep inside the codebase, introduce an abstraction over it, migrate callers to the abstraction, then swap the implementation behind it. The attribution is worth getting right — the name was coined by Stacy Curl; Paul Hammant first detailed the technique publicly (blog, April 2007, from a 2005 ThoughtWorks engagement at Bank of America); Fowler's bliki (2014) popularized it; Jez Humble's write-up of swapping iBATIS→Hibernate inside the Go CD tool is the standard worked example. For agents this is the single most mechanical pattern of the set: enumerate callers, introduce the interface, migrate call sites — repetitive, verifiable, diff-at-a-time work.

Expand/contract — also known as parallel change (Danilo Sato on Fowler's bliki, 2014; first documented as a refactoring strategy by Joshua Kerievsky, 2006): "a pattern to implement backward-incompatible changes to an interface in a safe manner, by breaking the change into three distinct phases: expand, migrate, and contract." It is the only sane way to evolve schemas and public APIs while both worlds run, and it is a key component of evolutionary database design — which is where §8 takes it. Dark launching (Fowler bliki, 2020) is the verification half of the toolbox: take the new behaviour and call it invisibly from production traffic — "the old and new code can both be called and their results checked… but only one answer returned to the interface." For a strangler cutover this is gold: the new slice serves real inputs in shadow mode, its outputs are diffed against the monolith's, and the cutover happens only after the diff is empty — B2's golden-master idea promoted from the test suite into production traffic. Feature flags (Pete Hodgson's canonical martinfowler.com article, 2017 — still titled Feature Toggles, whatever the industry calls them) supply the routing mechanism: release toggles decide per-request whether the facade sends this user, this tenant, this percentage of traffic to the old or the new side, which is what makes every slice reversible in minutes rather than deploys.

The toolbox is one idea wearing five costumes

Seam, ACL, branch by abstraction, expand/contract, dark launch + flags: all five exist to keep the two worlds decoupled enough that each step is small, verified and reversible. When a migration plan cannot name its seam, its translation layer, its routing mechanism and its shadow-verification step, it is not a strangler plan — it is a big-bang with better marketing.

4The heatmap: churn times complexity

The heatmap's premise is forensic: the code that matters is the code that changes. Adam Tornhill's Your Code as a Crime Scene (Pragmatic Bookshelf, 2015; 2nd ed. 2024) operationalized it: mine version-control history for change frequency per file, cross it with a complexity measure of the current snapshot, and the intersection — complex code that changes often — is the hotspot, where maintenance effort and defects concentrate. The idea has genuine academic roots independent of Tornhill's company: Mockus & Weiss predicted change risk from history at Bell Labs in 2000, and Nagappan & Ball's Microsoft study (ICSE 2005, Windows Server 2003) showed relative code churn discriminates fault-prone from clean binaries at 89% accuracy — this is the honest citation for "churn predicts defects", not a vendor benchmark.

The headline business numbers come from Tornhill & Borg's Code Red study (TechDebt '22, arXiv:2203.04374; 39 proprietary codebases, 30,737 files, Jira + VCS data): files in the worst code-health band carried 15 times more defects, and issues in them took on average 124% more time to resolve — print that as longer, not "124% faster" the other way, which is the marketing reframe — with nine times longer maximum cycle times, i.e. worst-case unpredictability. The caveats are not footnotes, they are part of the number: the study is correlational (the authors themselves ask whether bad code causes defects or defect-dense code reads as bad), all 39 codebases were customers of the authors' company, the Code Health metric is proprietary, and the conflict of interest is disclosed by the authors in a follow-up paper. Treat Code Red as peer-reviewed, vendor-affiliated, direction-confirming evidence. Two folklore corrections belong here too. The ubiquitous "hotspots are 4% of the code and hold 70%+ of defects" is a single-system anecdote from Tornhill's talks — his book says only that in one system hotspots were 4% of the code and contained 7 of the 8 most defect-dense modules; his SREcon 2024 figure is that hotspots (2–4% of the codebase) attract 20–70% of development activity. The defensible general statement is Pareto-shaped and independently replicated: roughly a fifth of modules carry roughly four-fifths of defects. And for the cost of the debt itself, the peer-reviewed figure is Besker, Martini & Bosch (JSS 2019): developers self-report ~23% of their time wasted on technical debt — prefer it over Stripe's vendor-commissioned 42% survey number.

For the agentic program the heatmap has a second, less obvious use: it is generated by exactly the read-only machinery B4 already built. Commit history, file sizes, dependency edges and complexity metrics are parser- and git-derived facts — the deterministic layer — so the heatmap is the one pilot-selection input that costs almost nothing, carries no hypothesis label, and can be regenerated weekly as the migration proceeds. It answers B6's F0 question "what do we point the net at first?" with data instead of with the loudest voice in the steering committee.

5Picking the first battlefield

Industry guidance on pilot selection is more specific — and more interesting — than the "start with something unimportant" folklore. Zhamak Dehghani's How to break a Monolith into Microservices (martinfowler.com, 2018) recommends warming up on "a simple and fairly decoupled capability" — one that does not require changes to many client-facing applications and ideally not even its own data store — because at the start "the delivery teams' biggest risk is failing to operate the microservices properly." Note what the criterion is: not low business value, but low coupling and low operational risk. And the same article then pivots: once the machinery works, extract what is important to the business and changes frequently — deliberately intersecting the commit-history hotspots with the product roadmap, because that is where extraction pays back fastest. AWS's prescriptive guidance adds operational criteria: few dependencies, release cadence, scaling needs, compliance scope, team cognitive load. The honest composite: first slice — decoupled, low-risk, machinery-proving; subsequent slices — high value, high churn.

Our workbook adds one criterion the industry lists do not, and it is exclusionary: business criticality vetoes the pilot, whatever the heatmap says. This is Report 4's synthesis, flagged as such, and the agent context is what makes it stricter than the classic advice. A human-led pilot that goes wrong produces a bad week; an agent-empowered team with write access producing changes at ten times the rate on the payments or safety module produces a blast radius no steering committee should accept before the harness — net, gate, review loop, rollback drills — has been proven on something the business can survive losing for a day. Criticality is not a weighted factor to be traded off against churn; it is a gate. A module that fails it is excluded, full stop; among the survivors, the heatmap and the coupling analysis decide.

The heatmap has a blind spot that a second axis fixes: it ranks how hot a module is, not whether it is a coherent thing to extract. A file can be hot, complex and still be a tangle of three domains sharing one class. Domain-driven design supplies the coherence axis — extract along bounded contexts and subdomains (Richardson's decompose-by-subdomain / decompose-by-business-capability patterns on microservices.io) — and event storming, the workshop format invented by Alberto Brandolini (earliest widely-cited write-up: Mathias Verraes, 2013), is the practical way to surface those boundaries in a brownfield system where the domain model lives in people's heads. The technique earns its place here because it converts B4's archaeology output into extraction candidates: the map says what exists, the storming sessions say where the domain seams run, the heatmap says what is hot, and the criticality gate says what is off-limits.

The decision rule assembles: run the criticality gate first; among survivors, require a seam (or a budgeted seam-manufacturing step); then rank by the weighted score, with heat and value pulling up and data coupling pulling down. The output is a shortlist of two or three candidates taken to the F0 exit review (B6), where the estate's seniors — the same people who graded B4's hypotheses — get the veto they have earned. A worked example: an invoicing-adjacent reporting module (hot, medium size, one database view, low criticality) beats the auth service (hot, small, but criticality-failing) and beats the pricing engine (hottest, but criticality-failing and a shared-schema tangle). The heatmap made the pricing engine visible; the gate and the second axis kept the program alive.

6Hyrum's Law and load-bearing bugs

The strangler's deepest trap is not architectural, it is epistemic: you cannot migrate a behaviour you do not know someone depends on. Hyrum's Law states it exactly (hyrumsław.com, observation by Hyrum Wright; named and popularized by Titus Winters; canonized in Software Engineering at Google, 2020): "With a sufficient number of users of an API, it does not matter what you promise in the contract: all observable behaviors of your system will be depended on by somebody." The essay is undated (online since at least 2017; widely cited as c. 2012 — no dated primary source exists, so cite it that way), and it names the two dependence classes that matter for migration: bug-for-bug compatibility — consumers depend on the bugs — and performance as an implicit interface, where consumers depend on timing the contract never promised. The obligatory cartoon is xkcd 1172 ("Workflow", 2013): every change breaks somebody's workflow. And the term "load-bearing bug" is engineering slang, not literature — use it freely, attribute it never.

The canonical war stories show the law operating at industrial scale. When Microsoft built Windows 95, SimCity's retail version used memory after freeing it — a bug that happened to work on DOS — so Windows 95 shipped an allocator that detected SimCity running and switched to a permissive mode (told by Spolsky in How Microsoft Lost the API War, 2004; corroborated by Raymond Chen's compatibility-lab accounts, where he took any application failing on Windows 95 as a personal failure). The Linux kernel enshrined the principle as its first rule: when a 2012 media-subsystem "fix" broke PulseAudio, Linus Torvalds overrode the maintainer's blame-the-userspace argument with the famous "WE DO NOT BREAK USERSPACE!" — and reverted, even though the userspace code was genuinely buggy. The lesson for a migration is symmetric: on a legacy estate, the consumers are often unknown integrations, cron jobs, Excel macros and other teams' workarounds — and every "obvious bug" is potentially somebody's dependency.

What Hyrum's Law implies for agent-driven change

Agents make drive-by fixes nearly free — "while you're in there, this null check is obviously wrong" — and Hyrum's Law makes drive-by fixes nearly uninsurable. The discipline: characterize before you fix (B2's net pins the current behaviour, bug included); check the consumers (B4's map shows who calls in); and ship the behaviour change as a deliberate, communicated migration with a flag and a rollback (§3), never as a side effect of an unrelated commit. The bug gets fixed on a Tuesday on purpose, with the integration teams warned — or it does not get fixed this quarter at all.

7God classes and the agent's context budget

The classic evidence against god classes predates LLMs and is stronger than most people realize. Khomh, Di Penta, Guéhéneuc & Antoniol (EMSE 2012; 54 releases of ArgoUML, Eclipse, Mylyn, Rhino) found that classes participating in antipatterns including the Blob/God Class are significantly more change- and fault-prone — and, crucially for the "it's just because they're big" objection, the effect persists after controlling for size. Abbes et al. (CSMR 2011) measured the comprehension side in controlled experiments: developers needed significantly more time and effort to complete comprehension tasks on Blob and spaghetti-code systems. God classes are where defects and comprehension cost concentrate — for humans, which is exactly why they also sit at the top of every heatmap.

For agents there is a second, newer penalty, and it should be stated with its evidence class attached. Liu et al., Lost in the Middle (TACL 2024): model performance "is often highest when relevant information occurs at the beginning or end of the input context, and significantly degrades when models must access relevant information in the middle" — drops of 20–30% and worse, in some configurations below the no-context baseline; the honest caveats are that these were retrieval/QA tasks on 2023-era models, and "U-shaped curve" is the field's shorthand, not the paper's phrase. Chroma's Context Rot technical report (July 2025 — vendor research, hedged, but with an open replication kit and directionally consistent with the peer-reviewed RULER and Levy et al., ACL 2024) generalized it across 18 models: "model performance consistently degrades with increasing input length", a single distractor measurably hurts, and the effect amplifies with length. Anthropic's own context-engineering guidance (September 2025, vendor) draws the operational conclusion: context is a "finite resource with diminishing marginal returns", and the goal is "the smallest possible set of high-signal tokens" — just-in-time retrieval, not whole-file stuffing. What does not exist, despite confident blog posts, is a peer-reviewed study correlating agent benchmark failures with target-file size — do not imply one; the argument is the synthesis: god classes force exactly the reading pattern (relevant lines mid-context, surrounded by distractors) that the long-context literature says models handle worst.

The practical consequence lands back on the heatmap and the pilot criteria. A 10,000-line god class is a triple liability: fault-concentrating (Khomh), comprehension-taxing for the human reviewers who must grade the agent's diffs (Abbes), and context-hostile for the agent itself (Liu/Chroma, with caveats). It should score high on the heatmap — it changes often, it hurts — but its size and tangle push it out of pilot territory and into a preparatory track: B4 archaeology to map it, B2 net to pin it, seam-manufacturing refactorings to split it (§3), and only then extraction. "Refactor the god class first" is not perfectionism; it is resizing the work to fit both the human review loop and the agent's context budget.

8The database is the hard part

Every strangler plan is elegant until it reaches the schema. Routing HTTP calls through a facade is a solved problem; splitting the data underneath them is not, because data has gravity, history and consumers nobody listed. Sam Newman's Monolith to Microservices (2019) is blunt about where the strangler's reversibility breaks down: the moment a migrated slice manages data that must be kept in sync between the monolith and the new service, each step stops being cleanly reversible, and the escape hatches are all compromises — a temporary shared database, a database view, a database-wrapping service, or change data capture. Chris Richardson's pattern catalog names the default failure: the shared database, whose costs are development-time coupling ("coordinate schema changes with the developers of other services") and runtime coupling — every service one slow query away from every other's outage. Dehghani's warning is the one to quote when a migration plan looks suspiciously clean: avoid the anti-pattern of only decoupling the facades and never decoupling the data — without decoupling the data, the architecture is not microservices, it is a monolith with network latency (and §10's distributed monolith is the name on the toe tag).

The mechanics of moving data safely are well documented. The dual-write problem (Gunnar Morling's canonical Debezium post, 2019): a service that writes to its database and publishes an event has no shared transaction across the two, so a crash between the writes corrupts the world silently; the outbox pattern fixes it by writing state and event into one table in one ACID transaction, with change data capture (Debezium tailing the write-ahead log) publishing asynchronously — CDC thus doubles as a database-level seam, letting the old and new worlds stay synchronized during coexistence without either knowing about the other. The full playbook is Stripe's Online migrations at scale (2017), the four-phase discipline Dehghani explicitly recommends: dual-write (via outbox), backfill history, run both and compare results, then cut over. Notice the family resemblance to §3: expand/contract for schemas, dark launching for reads, outbox for writes — the database is where all of §3's patterns get applied simultaneously, under less forgiving constraints. The pilot-selection consequence is already in §5's table: a module whose data cannot move (shared tables with twenty consumers) drops out of pilot territory no matter how hot it is; the ideal first battlefield owns its data or reads it through a single, severable view.

9What agents change in 2026 — and what they do not

The loudest recent claim in this space is Anthropic's How AI helps break the cost barrier to COBOL modernization (February 2026, vendor, point-in-time): modernization programs that took armies of consultants years can now run "in quarters instead of years", because the binding cost was never typing new code but understanding the old — and understanding is what agents reprice (the B4 thesis at industry scale). The market treated it as credible: IBM's stock dropped 13.2% the day it published, its worst single-day fall since 2000. Two details matter more than the headline. First, the post itself prescribes exactly this chapter's discipline — "start with a single component or workflow that has clear boundaries and moderate complexity… implement incrementally with testing at each step"; the strongest "AI changes the economics" source on record does not argue for big-bang anything. Second, the incumbents' rebuttal names precisely the work agents do not automate: IBM's Rob Thomas countered that "translation captures almost none of the actual complexity" — the hardware-software integration, data models, operations and behavioural equivalence that §6 and §8 are about. AWS Transform's re:Invent 2025 numbers (1.1 billion lines analyzed, 810,000 hours saved, customers at 1.5M lines/month) belong in the same bucket as B4's vendor file: unaudited, direction-confirming, magnitude-unproven.

The counterweight dataset is GitClear's analysis of 211 million changed lines (2020–2024, vendor but independent of the agent vendors): the share of changed lines that were moved/refactored collapsed from ~25% (2021) to under 10% (2024), copy-pasted lines rose from 8.3% to 12.3%, and churn climbed from 3.1% to 5.7%. Correlational, not causal — but the shape of the warning is clear: agents accelerate the production of new legacy at least as reliably as they accelerate migration. A strangler program without decommissioning discipline now accumulates zombie code faster than ever, which upgrades "the old system serves no traffic" from a nice-to-have to the program's central metric. The synthesis this chapter offers: agents make the heatmap cheap, not the big-bang safe. Discovery, dependency mapping, draft documentation and seam analysis — the inputs to §4 and §5 — collapse in cost; behavioural parity under Hyrum's Law, data migration, and organizational change do not. And one discovery finding closes the loop back to the harness: fitness functions (Ford, Parsons & Kua, Building Evolutionary Architectures, 2017) — automated architectural checks in CI — with a ratchet (ArchUnit's FreezingArchRule records existing violations, fails only on new ones, and shrinks the allowance as violations are fixed) keep the not-yet-migrated monolith from rotting further while the migration runs. A rule executable in CI is a rule an agent's every diff can be graded against; the ratchet is how you let agents touch the legacy code during a years-long strangler without letting them make it worse.

10Anti-patterns — how strangling goes wrong

The six failure modes
  • 1 · Piloting on the crown jewels. The payments/safety module is hot, visible and politically attractive — and one agent-accelerated bad week there ends the program. Criticality is a gate, not a weight (§5); the first battlefield is chosen to be survivable.
  • 2 · The permanent facade. The routing layer accretes logic, becomes a single point of failure and a performance bottleneck (Microsoft's own warning), and quietly turns into the new monolith's front half. Transitional architecture needs a decommissioning date, or a deliberate decision to keep it as a legacy-client adapter.
  • 3 · The strangler that never finishes. Practitioner folklore with no canonical source but universal recognition: the migration stalls at 60%, the dual-stack becomes permanent, and the organization now pays for two systems plus the sync glue between them. GitClear's rot data (§9) is the 2026 accelerant — agents produce new code faster than anyone decommissions old code. The metric that prevents it is decommissioned traffic and deleted lines, not migrated features.
  • 4 · The distributed monolith. Extracting along technical seams — shared database, chatty synchronous calls, lockstep deploys — yields a monolith with network latency (Newman). The bounded-context axis and the data-coupling criterion exist precisely to catch this before the first cut.
  • 5 · Drive-by fixes. "While the agent was in there, it fixed an obvious bug" — and Hyrum's Law collects the invoice from an integration nobody knew existed. Characterize first, migrate deliberately (§6); the bug fix ships as its own flagged, communicated change or not at all.
  • 6 · Facade-only decoupling. Clean API routing over a forever-shared database (Dehghani's named anti-pattern) — the diagram looks strangled, the schema says otherwise. No pilot whose data cannot move; outbox/CDC for the coexistence window; "database per service" as the explicit end state.

11Takeaways for our engagement

Assess (feeds B6/E7)
  • Is the pilot shortlist derived from the git-history heatmap plus B4's map — or from whoever argues loudest in the steering committee?
  • Which candidate modules fail the criticality gate outright? (Name them now, before they become tempting.)
  • For the surviving candidates: does each have a seam or a budgeted seam-manufacturing plan, and can its data actually move?
  • Do the domain boundaries exist on paper (bounded contexts, event-storming output), or is "the module" just a directory name?
Build (F1–F2)
  • The facade and the flags first: routing with per-request release toggles, so every slice is reversible in minutes (§3).
  • Dark-launch verification as the cutover gate: new slice shadows production traffic, outputs diffed, cutover only when the diff is empty.
  • Outbox + CDC for any module that must sync data during coexistence; shared database flagged as transitional with an exit date (§8).
  • Fitness-function ratchet in CI over the not-yet-migrated monolith: no new coupling to the parts being strangled, no new violations, agent diffs graded against the same rules (§9).
  • The program metric on the wall: old-system traffic and deleted lines trending to zero — not features shipped (§10).
Three sentences to remember B5 by

Big-bang rewrites concentrate risk into one irreversible cutover — Spolsky's Netscape is the canonical corpse and no honest failure-rate number exists — so the estate is strangled instead: a facade routes traffic, slices migrate one at a time behind flags, and every step ships verified against the old behaviour and stays reversible. The first battlefield is chosen by data, not enthusiasm: the git-history heatmap (churn × complexity, honestly caveated) finds what is hot, bounded contexts decide what is coherent, seams and movable data decide what is possible, and business criticality — our flagged synthesis — vetoes what is too dangerous for an agent-empowered pilot. Agents reprice the map, not the risk: they make archaeology, heatmaps and seam analysis nearly free (Anthropic's own COBOL playbook prescribes incremental migration), while Hyrum's Law, the database and the strangler-that-never-finishes remain exactly as dangerous as they were in 2004 — which is why the program measures decommissioned lines, and why the net, the gate and the ratchet come before the first cut.
