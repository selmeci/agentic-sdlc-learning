0Why it matters

B1 gave the safe ordering — read-only comprehension first, writes last — and B2/B3 built and graded the safety net. This chapter is the first phase done properly: what an agent produces when you point it at a legacy estate with no write access at all. The scenario is the one CROZ's mainframe practice states plainly: "You inherit a 10,000-line COBOL program with zero comments, written by someone who retired in 2005." Nobody left on the team can grade an explanation from memory, the documentation eroded decades ago, and the first profitable move is not to change anything but to understand — architecture maps, dependency graphs, convention inventories, ADR reconstruction, onboarding material. The risk ledger is the most asymmetric in agent adoption: a read-only mistake is a wrong sentence a human reads, not a wrong change a system runs. But that sentence is also the trap. Generated explanations have no compiler; a plausible-but-wrong account of legacy behaviour looks authoritative, gets cited, and quietly becomes load-bearing. So this chapter holds one line: the agent reads faster than any human and lies more fluently than any bad consultant — everything it produces ships as a labeled hypothesis, anchored in deterministic fact, until a person who can grade it promotes it.

1The comprehension bottleneck, measured

The claim that developers spend most of their time understanding code rather than writing it predates LLMs by decades, and for once the folklore has a measured spine. The strongest version is Xia et al.'s field study (IEEE TSE 44(10), 2018): not a survey but interaction data collected across all applications via their ActivitySpace framework — 78 professional developers, 7 real projects, 3,148 working hours. Verbatim from the abstract: "on average developers spend ∼58% of their time on program comprehension activities." Two details keep the number honest: navigation is counted separately (≈24%), not folded in; and senior developers spend significantly less of their time on comprehension than juniors — comprehension is a skill tax that experience partially pays down, which is precisely why an estate whose seniors have left is so expensive. The in-IDE predecessor (Minelli, Mocci & Lanza, ICPC 2015) put comprehension at roughly 70% of development time inside the IDE — the discrepancy with 58% is methodological (IDE-only with inferred gaps vs cross-application measurement), and citing both with their methods is worth more than averaging them.

The deep roots matter for the client pitch, because they show this is not an AI-era rationalization. Corbi's IBM Systems Journal paper ("Program understanding: Challenge for the 1990s", 28(2), 1989) already states the whole thesis in pre-web language: "Programmers have become part historian, part detective, and part clairvoyant." It reports Fjeldstad & Hamlen's maintenance study — programmers studied the original program "about three-and-a-half times as long as they studied the documentation," and just as long as they spent implementing the enhancement — and the FAA National Airspace System lesson that "the best way out is to relearn the old software, relying primarily on the source code," because the documentation had eroded past trust. Glass's 2001 IEEE Software fact list (the correct home of the widely misattributed "40–80%, average 60%, of software cost is maintenance") closes the loop: most money is spent on existing systems, and most of that spending is comprehension. Onboarding is the same cost concentrated: Sim & Holt (ICSE 1998) report the famous "six to twelve months to full productivity" — worth citing precisely, as one team's estimate inside a four-person case study — and Rastogi et al.'s Microsoft study (ESEM 2015, version-control data plus 411 surveyed new hires) found that poor documentation measurably delayed the first check-in (267 of 411 respondents; p<0.001), while its absolute ramp-up months are deliberately obscured for confidentiality — any blog quoting exact months from it is inventing them.

Why this is the agent-shaped hole

Every later phase of the bootstrap fights the agent's weaknesses — writing needs gates (E4), tests need grading (B3), permissions need enforcement (I2). Phase 0 is the only phase that rides the agent's strength: ingesting millions of tokens of code and restating their structure is exactly the cheap part, and its failure cost is a human's reading time, not a production incident. The economics argue the same way: if roughly half of engineering time is comprehension and legacy estates carry the worst comprehension debt, then a tool that even modestly compresses comprehension attacks the largest line item — with the smallest blast radius in the entire adoption roadmap. That is the whole case for starting here; the rest of the chapter is about not squandering it.

2The vendor evidence file, honestly weighed

The three flagship comprehension stories are all vendor or consultancy published. They are still worth knowing in detail — clients will quote them — provided each number keeps its hedge attached. ThoughtWorks CodeConcise is the richest. The primary technical account (Ferri, Coggrave & Sheth on martinfowler.com, September 2024) describes parsing the estate into a forest of ASTs stored in a Neo4j knowledge graph, enriching it with LLM-generated explanations at multiple depths, and retrieving through graph-traversal-augmented RAG — note the correction to our earlier notes: the system is explicitly a RAG system, with the graph making retrieval structural rather than replacing it. The engagement: an anonymized automotive manufacturer's backbone system, "15 million lines of COBOL" plus an IDMS database, built over three decades, with a hard licensing deadline. The numbers, with their tenses: reverse-engineering lead time per 10,000-line module went from 6 weeks to ~2 — stated as "we estimated" post-PoC in 2024 and as an achieved fact in the 2025 client story; the "60,000 person-days" headline and the technical article's "240 FTE-years" are the same projection in two units ("potential", "projected", "in the order of" — every primary instance hedged), extrapolated from a PoC on part of the estate, not a measured outcome; and the "fix in three hours that took three weeks" anecdote is from a different client, "in the best cases", with a ThoughtWorks pair driving the tool. The most load-bearing detail is one their own marketing buries: the client's earlier genAI reverse-engineering outputs were judged by technical SMEs to be only 60% accurate — which is why SME review was the bottleneck, and is the vendor's own inadvertent documentation of §4's failure mode. Point-in-time: CodeConcise is an internal accelerator, now folded into ThoughtWorks' "AI/works" platform — it was never a product you can buy.

IBM watsonx Code Assistant for Z at NOSI is the cleanest verbatim: "up to a 79% reduction in the time required for their Developers to understand their existing complex applications, in some cases reducing the time from 24 to approximately 5 hours" (IBM case study, 2025). Keep the wrapper: "up to", "in some cases", a two-application COBOL pilot at Egypt's National Organization for Social Insurance, published by IBM — and the case study's real punchline is mainframe retention (NOSI cancelled an off-platform migration and bought a z16), so the comprehension number sits inside a sales narrative. The product context moved fast: WCA for Z was superseded by "Project Bob" (unveiled October 2025, converging the Z and i assistants), GA March 2026, global availability announced 28 April 2026, with existing WCA clients "fully supported" on an adoption path — and Bob's heavy lifting is reportedly multi-model (Anthropic Claude in the routing mix), a quiet admission that frontier models overtook the in-house Granite stack. IBM's Bob-era marketing already offers a cautionary exhibit: one 2026 press release quotes APIS IT claiming "100% accuracy in documenting legacy JCL/PL/I systems" — a claim that should be read next to §3's measured precision figures and treated as a red flag, not a data point.

AWS Transform rounds out the trio. The durable usage figure is cumulative and analysis-scoped: "an estimated 1.1 billion lines of code" analyzed and "more than 810,000 hours of manual effort" saved (AWS, December 2025 — vendor-published, unaudited; the "~7 billion lines per year" figure circulating in secondary blogs pins to nothing and is dropped). The on-stage customer stories at re:Invent 2025 are more instructive than the aggregates. Western Union: a 35-year-old money-order platform, "just over 2.5 million" lines of COBOL, "what used to take over three months can now be achieved in six weeks from discovery to testing" — an ongoing pilot, partner-built, not a production migration. Unum is the story this chapter would have invented if it didn't exist: 1.5 million lines documented and business-rule-extracted "in a few hours" — and then the customer's own AVP states the thesis unprompted: "It is essential to understand what you are trying to modernize. Because if you cannot understand it, you cannot verify whether it is actually performing the same function after modernization." AWS's own architecture now explicitly mixes deterministic analysis with LLM agents, and its guidance is human-in-the-loop validation "continuously validated by domain experts" with testing consuming over half of project duration — the vendor's pipeline encodes §5's machinery. For budgeting, the only honest per-unit price found anywhere is GitHub/Microsoft's: AI-assisted legacy analysis runs "usually $2–5 per 1,000 lines analyzed" (github.blog, October 2025 — their framework is open-sourced as Azure-Samples/Legacy-Modernization-Agents, and their field guide insists human experts review the generated analysis).

How to read these numbers in front of a client

Four disciplines. Separate projection from measurement — 60,000 person-days is an extrapolation; 24→5 hours is "up to" and "in some cases". Ask what the unit is — 6 weeks is per-10k-module lead time including SME review, not effort-hours; "six weeks" at Western Union ends at testing, not production. Discount self-report — the METR RCT (B1) showed experienced developers finishing 19% slower while believing themselves 20% faster; any case study whose evidence is "developers said it saved time" inherits that gap. Flag the stage — customer quotes delivered from the vendor's own keynote are marketing even when the customer is real. None of this makes the stories useless: converging vendor investment plus §1's measured economics is decent evidence that comprehension is where the value concentrates. It makes them inadmissible as proof of how much.

3What the independent evidence says

The independent practitioner voice closest to the metal is CROZ, a Croatian consultancy that works with — but is not — IBM, and whose review of watsonx Code Assistant for Z (November 2025) is the most quotable honest assessment in the field: the Understand phase "is where I see the highest potential"; the tool should be used "as a documentation and understanding tool, not as an automatic translation engine"; and the explicit anti-pattern is "automated COBOL-to-Java conversion expecting production-ready output without refinement" — generated Java that is functionally plausible but "thinks in COBOL". Their verdict, "cautiously optimistic," is exactly the posture this chapter recommends, from a firm that sells modernization services and loses nothing by hyping. GitHub's own field guide (October 2025) is blunter from inside a vendor: "Everyone who's currently promising you, 'hey, I can solve all your mainframe problems with just one click' is lying to you."

The peer-reviewed measurements are sobering in a specific, usable way. Soliman & Keim (ICSA 2025) had 14 engineers pose 236 architecture questions about Apache HDFS to GPT-3.5 and graded answers against ground truth built from 46 architectural documents: average recall 0.594 but precision 0.395 — read that again as: more than half of what the model states about a real system's architecture is noise — with engineers rating quality and trustworthiness only "moderate", and the authors concluding LLMs are "not yet reliable enough to replace expert analysis." Siala & Lano (Frontiers in Computer Science, July 2025) compared LLM reverse engineering against deterministic model-driven reverse engineering on 20 programs: the untrained GPT-4 invented spurious constraints — invariants "more restrictive than those in the source code" — at a frequency of 0.43 (Python) and 0.67 (Java) per specification, where the deterministic tool produced zero (Java; 0.14 on Python). Their sentence belongs in every proposal: "It is difficult to automatically detect such errors, and hence correction of the output would need to involve human expertise." Amalfitano et al. (ECSA 2025) add the shape of the ceiling across four frontier models: high-level architectural styles recover passably, fine-grained patterns and semantic consistency do not. And the mutation-based stress test (arXiv:2504.04372; 10 models, 750,013 fault-localization tasks) shows the brittleness underneath the fluency: semantics-preserving changes to the code break previously correct model reasoning in 78% of cases — comprehension anchored in surface features, not semantics.

Honesty requires the other signs too. In education settings, GPT-3.5's code explanations were rated more accurate than students' (Leinonen et al., ITiCSE 2023); in a controlled IDE study, promptless LLM comprehension help beat web search for task completion (Nam et al., ICSE 2024, n=32 — with benefits differing sharply between students and professionals, which is the expert-sampling caveat in embryo); DORA 2024 modeled +25% AI adoption as associated with higher self-reported documentation quality (+7.5%); and RepoAgent's repository-level generated docs were blind-preferred over human-written ones (70%/91.33% on two open-source repos — a preference rate, not a correctness measure; citing it as accuracy is a trap). The measured error rates that do exist are dated and off-domain: Sarsa et al. (ICER 2022) found 67.2% of Codex's line-by-line explanations correct — a 2022 model on novice code. The gap is the finding: no peer-reviewed study measures the accuracy of LLM-generated documentation on proprietary legacy estates with expert verification. Any sentence of the form "generated docs on legacy code are X% wrong" is currently unpinnable; the honest claim is §4's mechanism plus §3's precision numbers from adjacent tasks — and it is why the workbook's own estate-level numbers must come from the client's sampling, not from a brochure.

4The failure mode: plausible but wrong

Simon Willison's much-quoted line needs its exact wording, because the nuance is the chapter. The post's title says hallucinations in code are "the least dangerous form of LLM mistakes"; the body says they are "the least harmful hallucinations you can encounter from a model" — and the argument is verifier asymmetry, not safety: "With code you get a powerful form of fact checking for free. Run the code, see if it works." The same post hands this chapter its counter-quote twice over: prose hallucinations demand "a critical eye, strong intuitions and well developed fact checking skills", and generated code itself "will usually look fantastic: good variable names, convincing comments, clear type annotations… This can lull you into a false sense of security." Chris Krycho's reply (March 2025) sharpens the point from the practitioner side: if the model gets "this most basic, statically knowable thing wrong, they will certainly get other, subtler things wrong as well" — the errors that matter are the ones no static check catches. Documentation of a legacy estate is precisely that category: prose about behaviour, authored fluently, with no compiler and — on an estate whose authors retired in 2005 — no resident oracle either.

The freshest field report reads like a controlled experiment for this chapter. "The Archaeologist's Copilot" (Nik Malykhin, martinfowler.com, July 2026) documents restoring a 2005-era Java 1.5/Ant "big ball of mud" with LLM help. The early, conventionally-prompted attempts produced exactly our failure mode: asked to modernize the build, the model emitted a plausible modern build.gradle for a project whose real structure was Ant — "The result was a lie — and a structural lie at that… It was describing the reality it wanted to see, not the one that actually existed." Fowler's own blurb compresses it: "His early use of LLMs gave plausible answers that did not hold up in the codebase." Malykhin's diagnosis is the operational one: "AI defaults to optimism. When I ask 'How do I run this?', it assumes I can run it. In a restoration mission, optimism is fatal." And his "lying tests" discovery is the failure mode one level down: a catch (Exception e) { e.printStackTrace(); } in main() means the process exits 0 and the modern pipeline reports a green pass on a system that did nothing — generated comprehension that looks verified and is not.

Real damage from plausible generated content is documented, though the cleanest incidents sit one domain over. Cursor's AI support bot (April 2025) confidently invented a multi-device login policy; users complained and some cancelled before a cofounder publicly corrected it — a generated claim, believed because it was fluent and official-looking, with no verifier in the loop. Inside software documentation proper, the evidence is structural rather than incident-based: Uddin & Robillard's study of 323 IBM professionals (IEEE Software 2015) found incorrectness of content among the severest documentation problems — rare but "blocker"-grade, driving developers to abandon the documented API entirely; Wen et al. (ICPC 2019) measured why staleness is the default, with only ~13–20% of code changes triggering any comment update; and Tan et al.'s iComment (SOSP 2007) showed wrong comments are not harmless, detecting 60 comment-code inconsistencies and 33 real bugs hiding behind them in Linux, Mozilla, Wine and Apache. The compound lesson: humans already fail to keep documentation true; a generator that produces authoritative prose at industrial scale without a verification loop does not close that gap — it automates it.

The asymmetry to internalize

On greenfield work the asymmetry bites less, because the author of the code is present to smell a wrong explanation. On a legacy estate the verifier hierarchy collapses: the code is the only trustworthy document (Corbi, 1989), the tests that could grade claims about it do not exist yet (they are B2's job), and the people who could grade from memory are gone. That is why the discipline of §5 is not optional polish on a productivity win — it is the difference between an archaeology program and a fiction generator with excellent typography.

5The validation machinery

Four mechanisms, layered. None of them is exotic; the assembly is ours, and §5's framing — every generated claim ships as a hypothesis with a confidence label until promoted — should be flagged exactly like D1–D5: we searched for an established practice of confidence-labeled generated documentation and found none (the nearest shipping analog, Swimm's CI status, labels staleness against code, not truthfulness of prose). The confidence label is not decoration; it is what makes the artifact reviewable. "Derived, unverified" can be read with profit; "documentation" cannot be told apart from truth by a newcomer.

Anchoring in deterministic fact. Every claim that can be derived by a parser instead of a language model should be: the dependency graph, the module map, the call structure, the build topology. This is the technique underneath the credible tools — aider's repo map (tree-sitter parses the repo, a graph-ranking algorithm budgets it into tokens — the map is parser-derived, the LLM only reads it), RepoAgent's AST-parsed, topologically-ordered doc generation, CodeConcise's Neo4j graph, and AWS's stated "combination of deterministic approaches for understanding, then combining that with AI." The LLM's role is interpretation on top of extracted structure, never extraction by recall. (A point-in-time caution against overclaiming industry convergence: GitHub's stack-graphs — the flagship deterministic name-binding graph — is now an archived, unmaintained repo.) Anchoring gives the reviewer a cheap oracle: a claim that disagrees with the graph is wrong deterministically; a claim that agrees is still a hypothesis, but a checkable one.

Expert sampling. Full review of generated volume is impossible by construction — generation is free, attention is not — so review must be sampled where it pays: critical modules, high-confidence-but-load-bearing claims, and everything destined for promotion. The only principled sampling machinery we found lives one field over (HADAS, arXiv:2404.01588: diversity-aware selection of likely hallucinations for human annotation) — borrow the idea, cite the provenance. Soliman & Keim's 0.395 precision is the quantitative argument that sampling is not optional; the CodeConcise 60%-accuracy anecdote is the vendor's own confirmation. Remember Nam et al.'s finding that benefits and failure modes differ between juniors and professionals: the sampler must be the estate's senior, which is also the political move — F0's review loop is where a defensive team learns the agent's outputs on questions they can grade (B1).

Executable cross-checks. Where a claim is checkable, check it mechanically and continuously. The mature school is docs-as-tests: Spring REST Docs has guaranteed since 2014 that "if a snippet is incorrect, the test that produces it fails"; Doc Detective and Manny Silva's "Docs as Tests" methodology run documentation procedures against the real product (note: a single-practitioner ecosystem — methodology, tool, book and case studies all trace to Silva; the Kong 84%→91% chatbot-accuracy vignette is his number, practitioner-sourced); Schemathesis derives property-based tests from the OpenAPI spec to catch doc-implementation drift. For architecture prose the strongest cross-check is the one this workbook builds anyway: B2's characterization net. "The doc claims module X recomputes Y nightly" is a hypothesis until a golden-master test pins the behaviour; every generated claim that survives contact with a pinned test earns promotion with evidence, not with prose review alone. That is the concrete meaning of the B2 handshake: archaeology proposes, the net disposes.

Flagged as our synthesis

The hypothesis-with-confidence label, the sampling protocol, and the promote-through-verification path are this workbook's Report 4 synthesis. The components are standard and named — parser-derived anchors (aider, RepoAgent), executable docs (Spring REST Docs, Doc Detective, Schemathesis), the derived-until-promoted grey zone (I1) and its freshness policy (I3) — but no external source assembles them into a validation pipeline for generated legacy documentation; our discovery pass found the territory genuinely unclaimed. The field is converging independently: AgenticAKM (arXiv:2602.04445, February 2026) generates ADRs from repositories with a dedicated validation agent in the loop — cite that as convergence, present the pipeline as ours.

Three operational lessons from the field reports belong in the runbook. Prompt as an archaeologist, not a tourist. Malykhin's "Tourist Prompt" — the naive "how do I run this?" — invites the optimism that fabricates a Maven layout; his "Archaeologist Prompt" assigns a forensic posture ("perform a Forensic Code Audit"; senior-legacy-architect persona) and, critically, demands evidence: claims must cite files and lines. An explanation that must point at its exhibit cannot drift as far from the codebase — and the citations are machine-checkable, which is anchoring at the prompt layer. Rebuild the era before interpreting it. The "time capsule" — a container with the period toolchain (Java 6 + Ant, zero code changes) — converts untestable prose about behaviour into executable questions, and flushes out the lying tests before the pipeline trusts any green. Scan for secrets before the agent reads anything at scale (gitleaks over full history — B6 owns the details): comprehension tooling ingests everything, and legacy estates are where the live 2009 passwords live.

The exit criteria are deterministic and short: every shipped claim carries a confidence label; expert sampling on the critical modules is signed off by the estate's seniors; the pilot-candidate shortlist is scored and handed to B5; and the entry-points package is accepted by B2 as generation input. What F0 deliberately does not produce: fixes, refactors, "modernized" build files, or documentation declared final. The artifact status stays I1's grey zone — derived until deliberately promoted — and promotion is a PR with a named human approver, never a bulk commit of generated prose.

7Anti-patterns — how archaeology becomes fiction at scale

The six failure modes
  • 1 · Shipping hypotheses as documentation. The label is the artifact; a generated map committed without its confidence status is §4 with a head start. First wrong decision taken on it — a refactor scoped by a fictional module boundary — costs more than the entire F0 saved.
  • 2 · Big-bang documentation. Generating prose for 15M lines because generation is free. Review bandwidth is the binding constraint — CodeConcise's own story has SME review as the bottleneck at 60% accuracy. Document what the heatmap and the team will actually consume; the rest stays derived-on-demand.
  • 3 · Tourist prompting. Optimistic, context-free questions get optimistic, context-free answers — the fabricated build.gradle. Forensic posture plus mandatory file-and-line citations, or don't bother.
  • 4 · Vendor-metric laundering. "79% faster comprehension" migrating from an IBM case study into your business case without its "up to / in some cases / two-app pilot / vendor-published" wrapper — or APIS IT's "100% accuracy" quoted straight. The METR gap (19% slower while feeling 20% faster) disciplines every self-reported saving.
  • 5 · Transformation-first. Skipping understanding for autonomous COBOL→Java because the keynote showed it. CROZ's verdict stands: output that "thinks in COBOL", needing refinement; Unum's AVP supplies the reason — you cannot verify equivalence against a system you do not understand. Comprehension is the prerequisite, not the optional first module.
  • 6 · Phase-0-forever. The mirror failure (B1's WO4): archaeology as eternal analysis paralysis, docs accreting, no net, no gate, no writes. F0 is time-boxed with deterministic exit criteria (§6) precisely because comprehension is comfortable — and comfort is not the engagement.

8Applying it — decision guide

Assess (feeds B5/E7)
  • Who can still grade an explanation of the critical modules from memory? (If the answer is "nobody", every generated doc is unverifiable by review alone — budget for executable checks.)
  • What deterministic extraction already exists — build graphs, static analysis, a repo map? Anchoring starts there, not with a prompt.
  • What does the estate's documentation drift look like — when did a human last update it, and would anyone notice a wrong paragraph? (Wen's 13–20% co-evolution says: assume stale.)
Build (F0, read-only)
  • Deterministic layer first: dependency graph + repo map by parser, secrets scan before the agent ingests history.
  • Hypothesis register with confidence labels as the single output format — promotion only via PR with a named approver (I1/I2).
  • Archaeologist prompts: forensic audit framing, mandatory file-and-line citations; time-capsule environment for behavioural questions.
  • Exit package: validated entry points → B2; scored pilot candidates → B5; sampling sign-off by the estate's seniors.
Three sentences to remember B4 by

Comprehension is where the measured time goes (58% of it, field-measured), where the safest ROI lives, and where the agent's strength — reading at scale — meets the estate's deepest debt; vendor stories (CodeConcise's two-thirds, NOSI's 24→5 hours) support the direction with every hedge attached, never the magnitude. The failure mode has no compiler: generated explanations are fluent, authoritative and — on the independent measurements — roughly half noise at architecture depth, so every claim ships as a labeled hypothesis anchored in parser-derived fact, sampled by the seniors who can grade it, and cross-checked against the characterization net before promotion. Do F0 as an archaeologist (evidence-citing, era-faithful, read-only) and it feeds B2's net and B5's heatmap; do it as a tourist and you have industrialized the production of confident fiction — which is worse than the documentation debt you started with.
